The Problem

The Streaming Trap

"Information is power. But like all power, there are those who want to keep it for themselves."

— Aaron Swartz, Guerrilla Open Access Manifesto (2008)

Anyone who has spent time on the internet has invariably come across a paywall. The growth of the streaming service model has led to steadily higher subscription prices — a Forbes survey found that 44% of Americans saw a price bump in the past year alone. With average internet costs and multiple subscriptions, some Americans are spending more than $110 per month just to access content.

But cost is only part of the problem. The dispersion of TV shows, movies, and live sports across dozens of competing platforms creates confusion and fatigue. Your favorite show might vanish overnight — platforms routinely purge content to cut licensing costs. Physical media is no safer, plagued by rights negotiations and dwindling availability.

Streaming services are only one piece of the puzzle. Corporate interests have seized upon our hunger for content, commodifying everything from academic articles to basic software. This hostage situation continues for as long as we open our wallets.

Our Mission

The Open Access Alternative

"You get to feed at this banquet of knowledge while the rest of the world is locked out. But you need not — indeed, morally, you cannot — keep this privilege for yourselves. You have a duty to share it with the world."

— Aaron Swartz, Guerrilla Open Access Manifesto (2008)

Aaron Swartz — architect of Creative Commons and a founding figure of the Open Access Movement — fought to liberate information from corporate control and paid dearly for it. From 2011 until his death in early 2013, Swartz faced state and federal charges for using MIT's network to download thousands of academic articles from JSTOR. His belief was simple: sharing knowledge is a moral imperative.

Swartz was not the first to act on this belief. In May 1970, 150 students at NYU "kidnapped" a six-ton mainframe belonging to the Atomic Energy Commission, demanding justice for local activists. Contemporary organizers in Amsterdam, facing extralegal AI surveillance from their own government, shared their story nationally — forcing the state to answer publicly.

Information can be given and taken away. Wherever it goes, it should benefit us all. Our project centers on one concrete way to embody that principle: the personal media server. By identifying legal, trustworthy sources of freely available media and providing a clear guide to hosting it yourself, we offer a small but meaningful act of information autonomy.

How It Works

What We Built

We built and documented a working personal media server using a Raspberry Pi and Jellyfin, a free and open-source media platform. The server hosts public domain films, audiobooks, and other freely licensed content sourced from the Internet Archive, Librivox, Project Gutenberg, and more.

Our build guide walks through every step — from flashing the OS to remote access via Tailscale — in plain language accessible to anyone. No prior Linux experience required.

Step-by-Step

Build Your
Own Server

A complete guide to setting up a Raspberry Pi media server with Jellyfin. No prior Linux experience required.

What You'll Need

  • Raspberry Pi 4 or 5 (4GB RAM min, 8GB recommended) ~$55–80
  • MicroSD card (32GB+, Class 10) — for the OS ~$10
  • External USB hard drive — for your media varies
  • Official Raspberry Pi power supply ~$12
  • Ethernet cable ~$5

Total: ~$100–150 one-time. No subscription fees.

01

Hardware & OS Setup

  • Download and install Raspberry Pi Imager
  • Flash Raspberry Pi OS Lite (64-bit) to your microSD card
  • Before flashing, click Edit Settings — set a username/password, enable SSH, enter your Wi-Fi credentials
  • Insert the SD card and power on the Pi
02

Remote Access

Option A — SSH

ssh yourname@raspberrypi.local

Option B — Raspberry Pi Connect

sudo apt install rpi-connect
rpi-connect on
  • Visit connect.raspberrypi.com to link your Pi and access it from a browser
03

Install Jellyfin

sudo apt update && sudo apt upgrade -y
curl https://repo.jellyfin.org/install-jellyfin.py | sudo python3
  • Accept defaults when prompted
  • Verify it's running: sudo systemctl status jellyfin
  • You should see active (running)
  • Jellyfin Linux install docs →
04

Access the Dashboard

  • Open a browser on any device on your home network
  • Go to http://raspberrypi.local:8096
  • Complete the Jellyfin setup wizard — create an admin account and add your media folders
05

Adding Media

  • Connect a USB hard drive for your media library
  • Organize files so Jellyfin can identify them:
    Movies/Movie Name (Year)/file.mp4
    TV Shows/Show Name/Season 01/episode.mp4

Transferring files to the Pi

  • Terminal: scp /path/to/file yourname@raspberrypi.local:/media/
  • Or use Cyberduck — a free drag-and-drop GUI
06

Watching Your Media

  • Browser — go to http://raspberrypi.local:8096 on any device on your home Wi-Fi
  • Jellyfin app — free on iOS, Android, Apple TV, Roku, Fire TV, Samsung TV, and desktop
07

Access Outside Your Home

Option A — Tailscale (Recommended)

curl -fsSL https://tailscale.com/install.sh | sh
sudo tailscale up
  • Install Tailscale on your phone or laptop and sign in with the same account
  • Access your Pi from anywhere as if you were home

Option B — Port Forwarding

  • Log into your router (usually http://192.168.1.1)
  • Forward port 8096 to your Pi's local IP
08

Where to Get Legal Media

  • Internet Archive — movies, TV, books, audio — all public domain
  • Wikimedia Commons — public domain films and historical footage
  • Librivox — free public domain audiobooks
  • Project Gutenberg — over 70,000 free ebooks
  • NASA Media Library — public domain photos and video
We only recommend using your media server with legally obtained or public domain content.
